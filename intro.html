The invisible border

Italy's life expectancy gap

At the two extremities of the Italian peninsula are Bolzano, the northernmost province, and Sicily, a Mediterranean island off the coast of Tunisia. This distance is not merely physical – Bolzano and Sicily also find themselves at the opposing ends of the EU’s poverty rankings. Sicily, at 52%, has the highest poverty rate in the European Union, while Bolzano, at 8.5%, has the lowest. The average disposable income for a Sicilian household is €12,500; a family in Bolzano has almost twice as much.

A boy born in Palermo, Sicily, has an average life expectancy of 79 years. The same boy born in Bolzano will live around 2 years longer. While girls may fare a little better, this gap is wider: 83 years in Palermo, 86 in Bolzano.

For centuries before Italian reunification, the story of Bolzano and Palermo was one of two different countries. The borders may be no longer there, but the spectre remains.

An invisible line cuts across the Italian peninsula. This border is the product of opinions, cultures, identities and self-perceptions. Sometimes it’s above Lazio, bordering the green hills of Tuscany. More often it is right in the middle, between Lazio and Campania.

Below this invisible border is the Mezzogiorno, a term that encompasses the whole of Southern Italy and Sardinia. It means “noon” in English and refers to the position and intensity of sunshine in Southern Italy at midday. In time, this geographical label has almost become an identity, so strong there is no corresponding definition for the other parts of Italy. It’s just the Mezzogiorno, and the rest.

It has also been, and still is, a pressing political problem. The Southern question, which generations of politicians have tried to answer, is no more than this: why is the Mezzogiorno so poor and backward? What can be done to erase this gap?

A never-ending crisis

The north-south economic gap has been an inaugural feature of the developing Italian state – resulting in an ever-broadening wellbeing gap.

With a median income almost half of their northern counterparts, households in the south are at a higher risk of poverty. Though most of the population lives in the North, nearly half of households in poverty live in the Mezzogiorno. The South of Italy is one of the poorest areas in the European Union, on par with the Southern Spain and parts of eastern Europe.

Economic disparities compound an unequal income distribution even further. More than half of the population in the south and islands inhabits the two lower income quintiles. Average disposable income is around 60% that of the North and has been so since 2004. Incomes have grown by about 10% since the early 2000s but have stagnated since 2008.

The economic crisis dealt a particularly hard blow the Italian economy, with the Mezzogiorno bearing a heavy burden. Recovery has been difficult and disproportionate, and the gap between North and South, if anything, continues to grow. Poverty has doubled compared to pre-crisis levels, with one in ten southern households living below the poverty line. Unemployment, which had declined significantly up until 2009, is now back to the early 2000s levels, and exceeds 20% in Campania, Calabria and Sicily.

For young people growing up in the South, the crisis isn’t over. And they feel it will never end. Almost one third of those aged 15 to 24 living in Sicily, Calabria and Campania are NEET, (not in employment, education or training). In these regions youth unemployment exceeds 50%.

“The crisis has been there for as long as I can remember,” says A., 24 years old, Cagliari. She is a graduate in foreign languages but is struggling to find a job in her field. She’s worked a part-time job at a restaurant to support her studies. “My family and I have sacrificed a lot for this degree. I don’t want this to go to waste. I don’t know what to do, I’d like to move to Milan and look for a job there, but it’s so expensive. I wouldn’t want to live my family, the city I grew up in, but I see no future here.”

Prophecy by postcode

Numbers are informative, but they can feel a bit dry. So, how do they translate into years, and health, lost? Socio-economic background and living standards are strictly related. According to the World Health Organisation, noncommunicable diseases, like cancer and heart conditions, are becoming more common. They cause more than 70% of deaths worldwide, and 88% in countries with higher incomes, where they disproportionately affect poorer groups.

Individuals with lower income are more vulnerable to illness and trauma that might otherwise be avoidable. A lack of access to health care (or inadequate healthcare), means people tend to get sicker, more frequently, their symptoms tend to be more intractable and thus they are more likely to die, because of diminished access to regular check-ups. People of the south are often more susceptible to lifestyle-related diseases, which can be the product of a lower economic background. Lack of access to cheap and healthy food options, for example, correlates with malnutrition and excess weight. These, in turn, increase the risk for a range of chronic conditions, like type 2 diabetes. In the workplace, individuals may be exposed to health hazards without any protection.

It is no coincidence that people living in the Mezzogiorno will live shorter lives, and are typically less healthy than average. The gap in life expectancy is around 2 years, but that in healthy life expectancy – that is, without disabilities and functional limitations – is a lot wider. In 2000, a newborn child could expect to live almost 50 healthy years if he or she was born in Sicily or Sardinia, and about 51 if they in Lombardy or Piedmont. By 2013, the gap had widened to 4 years: 56 in the Mezzogiorno, and 60 in the North. Life expectancy in the South has grown 30% slower than in the North.

In addition, more people also report being in bad health: almost 1 in 10 people over 14 in the Mezzogiorno have reported a physical or mental health condition - twice as many as in the North.

How to save a life

All these factors – lifestyle, environment, prevention, quality of healthcare – have profound negative effects on healthcare outcomes. When combined, they can be deadly, resulting in a stagnating life expectancy, and in deaths that might have been prevented, with adequate planning and public health policies.

The Italian research institute Nebo Ricerche PA uses national and EU data to produce data on avoidable mortality at the regional and province level. The team employs a methodology in which healthcare outcomes are weighted by age: deaths at a younger age count more towards the final score. The result is almost a health ranking - though the goal is not to shame health authorities, according to Nebo Ricerche’s scientific director Dr. Natalia Buzzi.

The institute flags public health issues and encourages the Italian NHS to critically assess its prevention and care strategies. “We want to draw attention to these issues,” Dr. Buzzi explains. “Why do some areas perform consistently worse than others, what methods can they borrow from others with significantly better rates?”.

Preventable deaths they still make up a majority of deaths up to 75 years of age. Of the 153,000 deaths in 2016, 101,000 were classed as avoidable, resulting in a remarkable loss of potential life expectancy of almost 20 days per capita.

The institute identified three main areas of focus: prevention through lifestyle, prevention through early diagnosis, and prevention through care and therapy. Lifestyle is mostly down to the individual choices and opportunities, while ensuring good structures and optimal efficiency of healthcare systems is gift of healthcare authorities, and key in ensuring high-quality diagnosis and care.

The data shows a stark North-South gap in amenable deaths, or deaths that could have been prevented with medical care. These account for a third of all avoidable deaths. Predictably, the regions of the Mezzogiorno record the majority of the worst rates in the country. While the north does have isolated areas of poor performance, it should be noted that changes in the latter may not be statistically significant due to the small numbers involved.

This indicator strongly correlates with the organisation of local healthcare precincts, Dr. Buzzi notes. “The NHS’ mission is to guarantee universal care to all citizens, regardless of where they live. Instead, the data suggests there is a significant regional divide in the quality and effectiveness of the healthcare system.” And even though preventable deaths have declined in the last years, progress has been slower in the south.

As Dr. Buzzi highlights, the North-South gap in preventable deaths is consistent with several health and economic phenomena, where the South is systematically at a disadvantage. Unfortunately, while preventable deaths are becoming a priority across the European Union, the issue isn’t a high on the agenda of Italy’s politicians. Several regional governments and local health authorities are using MEVI data to shape and revise their prevention strategy; meanwhile in Rome, Nebo's reports fall on deaf ears.

Screenings and awareness campaigns may cost money, but they are still a lot cheaper than therapy. “They say an ounce of prevention is better than a pound of cure, and it’s not just a figure of speech,” emphasises Dr. Buzzi. “The budget savings for the healthcare system are enormous. Not to mention the human costs.”

Intelligence and planning could go a long way in saving at least some of these lives in the future. But without proper funding, it won’t be possible to support a proper long-term prevention strategy.
