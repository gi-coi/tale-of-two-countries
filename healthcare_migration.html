Meet Italy's "healthcare tourists"

More than 300,000 Southern patients forced to migrate to receive treatment

Luca (not his real name) is a 10-year-old boy who lives in southern Italy. One day, his joints started to hurt, and before long he was unable to move. Diagnosing him was not straightforward, his doctor explains. “First of all, I ordered an x-ray. The nearest hospital turned him away, because the waiting lists were too long. It’s illegal, but when you’re the patient there isn’t much you can do.”

Luca would wait a month before finally getting x-rays at a hospital 150 kilometres away from his home. For a whole month Luca was on painkillers - his arms hurt so much he could barely move them. As a result of the X-ray doctors diagnosed he needed surgery but, there was another problem. “The doctors at that hospital refused to operate on him… they wouldn’t even say why. Apparently, no hospital in the region could treat him.”

They advised Luca travel to a hospital in the North-West of Italy. His mother immediately booked the plane tickets. Luca would be admitted there, but doctors requested new scans. Luca’s family was told the ones they already had were faulty. After repeating the x-rays, he was finally sent to surgery.

Luca is recovering, luckily he already feels a lot better; but it took two months, three different hospitals, two sets of x-rays and a 600-kilometre trip to across the country to receive treatment. “It’s disheartening,” his doctor comments. “This boy was in constant pain, and yet he couldn’t get the help he needed. He was caught up in a net of inefficiency and time-consuming bureaucracy. It’s unacceptable.”

Luca is one of about 500,000 patients who each year travel from the Mezzogiorno to the rest of the country to receive medical treatment. They are healthcare migrants: residents compelled to use healthcare services in a different region.

Low quality and mistrust are the biggest 'push' factors

Healthcare migration in Italy is partly the product of the historical migration flows, but it evolved into a separate phenomenon.

Italy is now known as an immigration hub, but this was not always the case. In the early 20th century, Italy was a land of emigration, and in many ways, it still is.

What became known as the “Italian diaspora” emerged shortly after unification. Between 1876 and 1914, almost 14 million people, mostly from the North-east, moved to continental Europe. By 1913, Southern Italy had overtaken and accounted for 47 percent of outflows. Most emigration from the Mezzogiorno, however, was transatlantic – mostly North and South America and Australia.

At this stage, migrants tended to be men, young and low-skilled leaving the country to find better economic conditions. Most emigrants were initially employed in mining and construction and would eventually be joined by high-skilled workers, often with their families.

The post-World War 2 economic expansion prompted a fresh wave of internal migration. Between 1952 and 1970, the average income per capita grew 130%. The North benefited the most from this accelerated growth, with the Mezzogiorno pegging behind.

At least 9 million people moved from the South to the North of Italy during this time, chasing the promise of the “economic miracle”. Many immigrated illegally, due to a fascist-era anti-urbanisation law abolished in 1961.

Far from stemming migration, this law only made it covert. Entire families left the Mezzogiorno and settled in the industrial North – especially the so-called Industrial Triangle area between Turin, Milan and Genoa.

According to the Italian research institute CENSIS, 1.5 million people were involved in healthcare migration. Of these, around 950,000 were patients, and 825,000 family members or friends accompanying them. Only a minority are “accidental” healthcare migrants: tourists, or people who moved but didn’t change their residence, fall in this category. Over 96% are people like Luca and his mother: people who are forced to travel to receive treatment.

The map of healthcare migration flows bears a striking resemblance to the history of Italian migrations. In addition to cross-border flows within the same area, at least 179,000 people moved from the South to the North in 2017; 152,000 from the South to the centre; 82,000 from the Centre to the North. CENSIS estimates suggest the numbers might be higher.

In total, around half a million people from the Mezzogiorno travelled for their medical care in 2017. The North-South route, on the other hand, is almost non-existent. This pattern is consistent with many migration flows in Italy. Given the disparities in the Italian healthcare system, this isn’t a surprise. Patients at hospitals in the South report consistently lower satisfaction rates than those hospitalised in the north. More than that, people don’t trust the public system and the quality of the services it provides.

“No, I don’t trust my local hospital so much, even though I don’t really go there that often,” says Claudia, 28, from Orani, Sardinia. “I realise it isn’t rational, but I guess negative memories or rumours stick more than happy endings. When your health is at stake, you don’t really want to take any chances. You may come across a good and caring doctor, or you may fall into the hands of someone who doesn’t care about you, doesn’t talk to you, doesn’t help you through your journey there. I’ve felt estranged – as if I wasn’t more than a number. And yet, my mum ended up in hospital last year, and she had a wonderful experience. It’s an irrational fear sometimes, it’s an instinct. You hear so many stories you end up losing trust in the doctors, in the system as a whole.”

The feeling of abandonment and mistrust is what pushes people to book a plane ticket or board their car, and travel hundreds of kilometres to find better treatments. The professional relationship between a patient and the medical staff is imbued with new resentments and emotions, especially when someone’s health is on the line. Southern patients’ experiences with and feelings towards the local healthcare system are one of the biggest ‘push’ factors in healthcare migration.

Who is migrating?

Giampietro calls himself a ‘healthcare tourist’.

“If you don’t laugh about it, what can you do?”. Giampietro, who is 27, has migrated across hospitals for most of his life. He has a chronic condition called cystic fibrosis, which requires constant monitoring and extensive care. When he was 15, he was admitted to the hospital in Nuoro, Sardinia, for a particularly aggressive chest infection. The doctors were puzzled. As he got worse, they eventually advised he and his family visit a specialist centre in Verona, in the north-west of Italy.

“I go there every year, twice a year now. I stay there for a couple of weeks at a time, I run all the tests, do my daily physiotherapy. I quite like it there, it’s like a small community. You get to know all the doctors and the nurses there, you become friends with other patients.”

Giampietro is a textbook healthcare migrant. Though cancer patients are a majority (43%), followed by patients with heart conditions (26%), those with chronic illnesses account for about a quarter of admissions (24%). Healthcare migrants cite the quality of care and long waiting lists as the main reasons to migrate. According to a 2014 study by CERGAS at the Bocconi university, they are also more likely to access private healthcare. While private and contracted facilities received about a quarter of all resident admissions, they accounted for nearly half of long-distance migrants’ admissions.

The framing of healthcare migration as a choice or “tourism” is inflammatory. After all, Italy should provide universal public healthcare for all citizens. Healthcare mobility is the pragmatic expression of a right enshrined in law: to guarantee citizens’ freedom to express their right to access to the highest level of care available.

With twenty regional healthcare systems offering widely different services and quality standards, healthcare migration has become a necessity, rather than a choice for some. Some hospitals have been forced to turn away patients like Luca, unable to provide the required care.

People with chronic conditions like Giampietro also struggle to receive appropriate care near their home. There are 20 cystic fibrosis centres in Italy – one for each region – but not all of them are equipped to deal with many complications of the condition. “With cystic fibrosis, every patient is unique. There are few of us living in Sardinia, and when I was first diagnosed, doctors were unfamiliar with cases like mine. They studied the theory, they read it on the books, but they had had few opportunities to put that knowledge into practice. Until a few years ago, my regional centre didn’t even have a physiotherapist, who is crucial for us.”

As a result of these disparities, patients tend to converge around centralised treatment locations, such as Verona, while the others treatment centres languish. Giampietro enjoys his time in Verona: it’s a pleasant break from daily life in Sardinia, a home away from home. “I do love it in Verona, and I’ve learnt to make the most of my time there. I’m also lucky because my health allows me to travel around, go to concerts and exhibitions, hop on a train and visit cities nearby. But for others, it is a source of unnecessary trauma and expenses.”

A necessity… or a privilege?

Healthcare migration is a cumbersome and stressful journey, for many – but it’s also an expensive one. Italian healthcare is free or requires a relatively small contribution – called “ticket”, but with transport, board and lodging and more on top of medical costs, expenses can add up quickly. During this time, they often have to take breaks from their job, in some cases becoming unemployed.

Costs vary significantly depending on the facility – public or private –, the length of the stay, the type of procedures required. Cancer patients, for example, may spend upwards of €1,700 per journey, and up to €7,000 a year, according to CENSIS. Chronic patients are also faced with high expenses, especially if they travel from isolated areas, like Sardinia. “I travel with my mum, and we spend €400 to €500 just for the plane tickets, if we’re lucky. In total, we spend €1,000, €1,500 every time. We go there twice, so €2,000 to €3,000 a year.”

In some cases, patients are entitled to partial refunds. When a given local health service is unable to provide a procedure, patients forced to travel to another region can seek compensation to cover the costs – but it is not guaranteed. “I work in Sardinia, where hospitals aren’t always equipped to treat more complex cases. I’ve had to send several patients to the mainland to receive treatment,” explains a paediatrician who spoke on condition of anonymity. “Sometimes the hospital staff themselves suggest parents take their children to another region. In that case, you can have a refund. But I know some parents who don’t apply for it, even if they’re entitled to it. Sometimes, especially when you’re a parent and you would do anything to make your child feel better, you’re not even thinking about bureaucracy. You don’t have time to walk into an office and file your request.”

Reinforcing inequalities

Healthcare mobility should encourage competition between regional healthcare systems. Less attractive providers should be compelled to invest more money into their services, improve their quality and treat more patients. When a resident in one region receives healthcare in another region, the latter accumulates a transfer of credit from the resident’s home region. Regional governments settle these flows through political and technical agreements.

This market system should work in theory, but in practice, it reinforces existing imbalances. Regional healthcare services in the Mezzogiorno owed nearly €1.2 billion in mobility debts in 2018, following a political settlement between regional governments. This compares to the significantly smaller net deficit of the Centre – €263 million – and almost mirrors the North’s net €1.1 billion surplus. The North funnels healthcare funding from the rest of Italy’s regional budgets.

Only 7 out of 20 providers recorded a surplus: five of them are in the North, two – Tuscany and Umbria – in the Centre, and only one – Molise – is in the South. Together, they held mobility credit for more than €2.8 billion – 60% of all total mobility credit generated in the country. Lombardy alone accumulated nearly €1.2 billion in credit and had the largest net migration surplus.

The regions with the highest mobility debt, (with the exception of Lazio) are all in the Mezzogiorno. Calabria’s deficit exceeds €302 million, and Campania’s is over €290 million.

The healthcare mobility market was worth around €4.6 billion in 2018 for hospitals, up from €3.9 billion in 2013. This would indicate patients are moving more or need costlier procedures.

In the absence of mechanisms to redress healthcare inequalities, the mobility credit system reinforces these disparities, benefiting richer, more attractive regions, and draining resources from those that are already underfunded.

With more resources in their budgets, these providers get additional funding on top of what they receive from the government. This can give providers the ability to circumvent public expenditure limits, redistribute resources, recruitment, upgrade services and facilities, or provide highly specialised care. According to a 2018 study, regional healthcare systems in the Mezzogiorno rely more on public funding than in the North, because they earn less from other sources – like interregional mobility.

Healthcare migration creates a vicious cycle for regional healthcare systems in the Mezzogiorno. They are charged with a double expenditure, having to fund their own providers while paying for services in other regions. They are forced to cut budgets for equipment, clinics, staff. This in turn means they become less attractive among residents. What is more, they are allocated less bedspace because they have negative net migration.

Hospital beds have been slashed by 75% since 1981. Between 2010 and 2017 alone, the total number of hospital beds was cut by almost 33,000 – 28,000 of which in the public sector. Calabria and Apulia cut more than 25% of their hospital beds. If the healthcare migrants trend continues to grow, the Mezzogiorno, the gap will worsen.

In 2017, the Regions and Autonomous Provinces’ Conference – a coordination mechanism between regional governments – agreed to introduce some form of compensation in overall funding to account for material deprivation and lower-than-average life expectancy. Some local governments, however, are advocating for stronger performance-based rewards, which go in the opposite direction.

Patients and their families end up suffering from this unequal mechanism. While some hospitals go deserted, others are over capacity. The Verona centre, for example, is not accepting new patients for the time being, which means people like Giampietro, with more severe forms of cystic fibrosis, will have to migrate elsewhere.

Cystic fibrosis is also an example of how inequalities can be overcome, with the right people. According to Dr. Carlo Castellani, specialist and director of the cystic fibrosis centre at the Gaslini hospital in Genoa, standards have vastly improved in the last twenty years. “Some centres made a political and strategic choice – networking with the latecomers, those facilities with fewer capabilities and expertise, to nurture that knowledge in-house. In time, these centres caught up with their more established partners, and they are now able to guarantee the same level of care. Now more and more patients are returning to the centres in their region.”

Some procedures, like lung transplant, are still rare in parts of the South; but as Dr. Castellani is keen to underline, it isn’t a reflection on doctors’ skills and performance. “There are cultural and social issues at play, like a very low number of donors in this case. The most significant problem is the lack of resources, which makes it impossible to plan radical overhauls and invest in innovation.” Giampietro, as someone with a chronic disease, is a so-called “expert patient”: he’s become very good at being his own doctor, in many ways. He also believes networking is key to improve care.

“For hospitals dealing with diseases like ours, it isn’t enough to study the subject in the books. You need to build a medical culture around it… It takes initiative from doctors, and willingness to grow talent and expertise in this subject. There are about 5,000 of us [with cystic fibrosis] in Italy. With well-functioning regional centres, each of us could receive care without having to travel long distances with our families.”

These knowledge networks benefit both doctors and patients. But these efforts alone cannot close the healthcare gap. A strong political commitment is needed from both the national and the regional governments. “We need a broader network with a strategic direction, and that can only come from the government,” explains Dr. Castellani. And of course we need more investments, because the healthcare system is starved for money, in the North and South alike."
