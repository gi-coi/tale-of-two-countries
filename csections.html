Curbing the "c-section epidemic"

What is happening in Southern Italy's delivery rooms?

Claudia was 18 when she had her first child.

“I had been in labour for six hours, but they couldn’t see the baby’s head," she recalls. "Then a doctor told me, ‘That’s it, we need to cut.’”

Claudia was brought into the operating room just after midnight. Soon after, she had a c-section and delivered her baby.

“They didn’t explain why they were doing it," she adds. " I had no idea what was going on. Six hours isn’t that long, is it?”

The experience scarred her and left her traumatised. In her second pregnancy in 2018, Claudia was determined to take control of her treatment.

“I told my gynaecologist I wanted to program a c-section," she says. "Initially he tried to persuade me otherwise, but then agreed with me and supported my decision.” Together they planned a date and time, scheduling the procedure during her doctor’s shift. When Claudia was admitted to the hospital a few months later, she was confident.

“Some nurses at the hospital tried to persuade me to try with a natural birth… But I didn’t want to. I was too frightened, to be honest,” she says.

Claudia’s story isn’t unique. Every year, thousands of women in the South of Italy have a c-section with no medical justification, facing longer recovery, as well as higher risks of complications and long-term side effects.

In what experts describe as a “c-section epidemic”, some clinics in Southern Italy are performing c-sections on up to 60% of women.

A global epidemic?

According to a 2018 study, the rate of c-sections worldwide has nearly doubled since the turn of the century, from 12% to 21% of all births in 2015. This is partly thanks to improvements in medical techniques, which have made the procedure safer and more accessible. Women are having kids later in life, and some may resort to IVF or similar procedures. These pregnancies are riskier for both the woman and the child and are more likely to require a caesarean.

The trend is raising alarm in the medical community, with concerns of c-sections for non-medical reasons. The World Health Organisation has issued new guidelines, recommending c-sections only when they are medically justified. While noting caesareans can improve survival in countries where rates are below 10%, it found that high rates do not correlate with better outcomes for women and babies.

In Italy, the c-section rate has trebled from 10% in 1980 to around a third of all births (33.8%) in 2017. Italy has one of the highest rates for caesarean deliveries in the EU, and the fifth highest in the OECD.

Biological and demographic factors certainly play a role - Italy’s fertility rate is the lowest in the EU, and women tend to have their first child in their 30s. These causes alone, however, cannot explain the huge gap between Trento, where only one in five women has a c-section, and Campania, where 50% of women do.

Smaller maternal units at higher risk

C-sections are also disproportionately more common in smaller maternal units, even though only a minority of high-risk and pre-term pregnancies happen there. For these hospitals, which are often less equipped to face medical emergencies and may not be staffed 24/7, c-sections are almost a way to make up for these shortcomings.

“I know of c-sections being planned around the staff’s shifts, to make sure the whole team is there for the surgery," doctor N., a paediatrician in rural Sardinia, explains. "That way, if something goes wrong, they can’t be accused of negligence. It’s a real issue when you don’t have a gynaecologist or an anaesthesiologist available, so you need to make do with what you have.”

Hospitals should guarantee a full medical team – gynaecologist, paediatrician, anaesthesiologist and obstetrician – around the clock, but it isn’t always the case.

Until a few years ago, small maternal units were far more common in the Mezzogiorno. A parliamentary commission found 86 hospitals in Italy accounted for just over 300 births every year. Half of them were in three Southern regions – Sicily, Apulia and Campania. Regional administrations agreed with the government they would close all maternal wards with fewer than 500 annual deliveries, with limited exceptions for those that served deprived or isolated areas.

Today, about a quarter of maternal care wards are classified as “small”. In 2015, these 117 units accounted for less than 5% of births. They are more common in isolated areas and in the Centre and South of Italy. On average, they have 190 to 226 deliveries per year – less than a baby every two days.

Many of these facilities don’t meet basic requirements. Only two of them have an intensive care unit (NICU). Some do not offer epidurals – an injection to reduce pain during labour – or rooming in for new moms and their babies.

“In smaller hospitals, staff members gain less practical experience than those in larger facilities, with more patients," Dr. Davide de Vita explains. "Some almost develop a ‘fear of the delivery room’. Vaginal births are less risky, but they can be more unpredictable, and if you don’t experience them on a daily basis, it’s difficult to factor in all the variables and make snap decisions.”

De Vita is a gynaecologist at a hospital in Ischia, Campania, and is the regional secretary of AOGOI, a gynaecologists’ and obstetricians’ scientific society. AOGOI has been at the forefront of efforts to curb the inappropriate use of c-sections, especially through training and awareness campaigns.

Defensive medicine

Unlike Claudia, Laura wasn’t a fan of c-sections. However, during her first pregnancy, she was scheduled for one.

“I was almost eight months pregnant, and the doctor told me my baby wasn’t growing and we needed to have a caesarean," she recalls. "I completely panicked. I asked him to wait another couple of weeks, see how it would progress. Two weeks later, he told me we couldn’t wait any longer. He was very supportive, he explained what the procedure would involve and the risks behind it… But I couldn’t think straight. I kept thinking ‘What if I die on the operating table?, Is the baby okay?’. It was awful. But I got through it.”

Ten years later, Laura was pregnant again. This time, she was optimistic.

“I felt really good about it," she says. "I thought I could have a ‘natural birth’, this time.” But one day, her gynaecologist told her there was a problem. Once again, the baby was too small. She was surprised, but she trusted her doctor's judgment. He knew what was best for her and her son.

She was also more prepared, this time. The procedure went smoothly and she recovered more quickly than her first time.

“I don’t know what they changed, but I’m glad they did,” she laughs. She glances at her baby, who is sleeping in the living room, now. He’s doing great and is growing fast.

“He’s growing much better than my first child, you see," she pauses. "It makes you wonder… Did I really need this surgery? Maybe, if I had been stronger, I could have requested a trial labour. But how could I know? I’m sure my doctor made the right call. You can’t really be too confident in these situations.”

“I feel doctors sometimes try to protect women from unnecessary stress,” Maria chimes in. She also had two c-sections almost ten years apart. “Maybe you could carry the pregnancy to term, go through 15 hours of labour and then they’ll rush you to the operating table for an emergency c-section. Instead, if you know what will happen, you can prepare yourself for it.”

Gynaecologists and obstetricians are under significant pressure to protect both the woman and the child, especially at critical moments like childbirth, where most of maternal and infant deaths occur. They face a higher legal risk than other specialists and they could be held personally liable in the most serious cases, like the death of the mother or child, or complications resulting in permanent damage.

This defensive approach to pregnancy can lead doctors to perform unnecessary medical procedures to give their patients some peace of mind, while protecting themselves and the hospital from scrutiny.

In doing so, doctors may in fact expose their patients to higher risks. Women who deliver with c-section have a slower recovery and are five times as likely to suffer serious complications during childbirth. They are also readmitted into hospital more often post discharge. Furthermore, they are at a higher risk of experiencing severe complications in subsequent pregnancies, like infection, uterine rupture and haemorrhage.

For the child, c-sections are associated with conditions like asthma and allergy in the short term, and obesity, high blood pressure and obesity later in life.

Financial gains for motherly pains

There is a less obvious, but equally compelling incentive to perform a c-section: financial gain.

Caesarean sections can be lucrative for a hospital. The Italian healthcare funding is tariff-based: providers are paid a fixed amount for each procedure they perform based on diagnosis-related groups (DRGs). The more complex a procedure and the hospital stay, the higher the refund to the hospital. As c-sections are more expensive and require longer hospitalisation, they are also paid more, regardless of their medical appropriateness. Hospitals receive €2,092 for a caesarean, versus “only” €1,272 for a vaginal birth with no complications.
A 2013 study of c-section appropriateness in Italian hospitals found private providers are especially receptive to this incentive.

In 2013, a government investigation revealed a number of hospitals submitted incomplete or altered medical records, making it very difficult to verify if a c-section was medically justified. The analysis concluded up to 43% of all c-sections performed could be unnecessary – that is, if they had been performed at all. In Sicily, inspectors found an anomalous number of medical records where the baby was breech, which is a common reason to perform a c-section. The discharge forms, however, did not list this information, suggesting some hospitals might have inflated the number of c-sections performed to demand a higher refund from the state.

This alleged abuse cost the Italian health service up to €85 million a year.

A matter of trust

“Pregnancy has become too medicalised. Women are being pushed out of the picture,” doctor N. notes. It’s a complex question: should a doctor refuse a woman’s request for an unjustified surgery? Which is more important: a woman’s agency over her pregnancy or scientific evidence?

However, how do women who opt for a c-section feel? Looking back upon it, Claudia isn’t so sure of her choice.

“Recovery is really hard after a c-section," she explains. "And I was in so much pain during the surgery as well, it wasn’t like that the first time… You know what? In retrospect, I wish I’d done it differently. ‘Maybe I could have tried’, I tell myself.”

And yet, a few months back, it wasn’t even an option.

“When you’ve already had a caesarean, doctors tend to be a lot more cautious with you," Claudia clarifies. "I chose a c-section because no other option seemed viable. Do I even know any women who had a natural birth after a c-section? I don’t, really. It is almost a default.”

In some northern hospitals, as many as 50% of women with prior c-sections eventually have a vaginal birth, versus 1 in 10 in the Mezzogiorno. The stark difference indicates that natural birth is far from being standard in the South, with both doctors and patients reluctant to have trial labour.

Even though it wasn’t medically justified, Claudia’s gynaecologist supported her decision.

“He was also a bit reluctant to let me have a ‘natural’ birth, so he didn’t push too much with that idea," Claudia explains. " I know if I had asked for it instead, he would have supported me regardless.”

And what if he hadn’t been on board with her c-section request? Claudia doesn't need to think about it.

“In that case, I would’ve probably looked for another doctor,” she answers without hesitation.

Bridging the gap: Campania leads the way

A medical culture prone to overtreating and medicalising pregnancy can condition women’s choices and preferences. Numbers can't measure the trust relationship betweeen patients and their doctors, but it plays a large role in healthcare, especially during a pregnancy.

"A gynaecologist isn't like any other doctor," Maria says. "They follow you from the first appointment till the birth. It almost feels like they're pregnant with you."

"When you have a bad experience with a gynaecologist, it really affects you on a personal level", Laura adds. "You really create an emotional bond with them. It doesn't happen as much with other doctors."

Claudia, Laura and Maria had very different experiences with pregnancies and c-sections. They did however, share one thing. All of them placed a great deal of trust in their gynaecologist: a doctor, but also someone who gave them care, advice, and emotional support. Understanding the synergy between patient and carer in this relationship is key to healthier and more safer pregnancies.

Gynaecologists are the first point of contact between a pregnant woman and the medical system. Educating and training practitioners helps both them and their patients realise that caesareans aren’t the only option – nor necessarily the safest one.

A few hospitals in the Mezzogiorno are reversing the c-section trend, and more women now are having a vaginal birth. In Campania, some clinics have cut their c-section rate by 20% between 2016 and 2017. Thanks to a mix of political will and pressure by the scientific community, hospitals are now taking steps to address malpractice.

Controlling the use of c-sections became a political priority for the regional government elected in Campania in 2016. The newly appointed administrative directors set strategic priorities and plans accordingly.

“There was a good synergy between national and regional strategic plans, consistently endorsed by the local scientific experts,” Dr. De Vita explains. AOGOI and its sister organisations organised theoretical and practical training courses, including case studies with mannequins and virtual simulations.

“By providing extensive training in smaller hospitals, we made them more secure," he adds. "That way, they can provide high-quality care, comply with regional and national standards, and resort to c-sections only when needed.” Public and private hospitals with higher-than-average rates received inspections and were requested to implemented specific childbirth plans that offered alternatives to c-sections. The regional government threatened to rescind conventions with the private providers that didn’t comply with the national and regional directives.

The regional government claims the c-section rate is as low as 36% now. This is much lower than the official data (53.7%), which is still almost a 10-point reduction in 2 years. If confirmed, these results could set an example for the rest of the Mezzogiorno, where rates are still well above the 10-15% recommended by the WHO. Investing in facilities, training and human resources would speed up progress, Dr. De Vita argues, but they aren’t always available. Seven regional healthcare systems (none of which are in the North) are undergoing budget restructuring programmes and have limited spending power. Unfortunately, political will alone can’t be enough to end the "c-section epidemic" – but it’s a good start.
